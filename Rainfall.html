  Rainfall is vital to maintaining the health of lakes and freshwater systems, impacting its water levels, quality, erosion, sedimentation, and biodiversity. Excess rainfall can lead to flooding, which introduces pollutants, sediments, and nutrients, degrading the water quality and disrupting the ecosystem. Floodwater can also erode the shoreline, which can increase sedimentation and introduce pathogens and contaminants. Conversely, droughts can lower the water level, reduce habitats, and concentrate pollutants, which can stress or eliminate species not adapted to those conditions. Factors like runoff, temperature changes, and chemical contaminants also impact water quality and the broader ecosystem.

  Rainfall also affects biodiversity by altering oxygen distribution levels, nutrient mixing, and the structure of the lake itself, which can disrupt breeding grounds and shelter. Additionally, sudden changes in water quality may favor invasive species over native ones. The impacts of rainfall are interconnected to the entire ecosystem, including surrounding land and wildlife. From nutrient distribution to habitat availability, rainfall significantly shapes the balance of freshwater ecosystems.
